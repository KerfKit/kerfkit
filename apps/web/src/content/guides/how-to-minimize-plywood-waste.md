Sheet goods are the most expensive raw material in most shops, and the offcut pile is where margins go to die. You can't get waste to zero — grain direction, kerf, and real-world part sizes see to that — but the gap between a careless layout and a planned one is routinely a whole sheet on a decent-sized project. Here's how to close that gap.

## 1. Design with the sheet in mind

Waste is mostly decided at the drawing stage, not at the saw.

- **Mind the magic dimensions.** A 4×8 sheet is nominally 1220 × 2440 mm. Parts at 300, 400, or 600 wide rip cleanly into that; parts at 410 or 620 leave strips too narrow to reuse. When a design allows ±10 mm, nudge toward divisions of the sheet.
- **Remember edge trim.** Factory edges are rarely straight or square, and shipping dings the corners. If you trim 5–10 mm per edge before cutting parts, your usable field is smaller than nominal — plan on it, don't discover it.
- **Watch grain-locked parts.** Doors, drawer fronts, and visible side panels usually must run grain one way. Locked parts pack worse — that's unavoidable — but knowing *which* parts are locked lets the layout compensate with the parts that can rotate.

## 2. Group cuts by thickness and material

Every material/thickness combination is its own packing problem. Cutting 18 mm carcass parts and 6 mm backs from the same mental pile guarantees confusion and orphan offcuts. Split the cut list by material first, optimize each separately, and buy sheets per group.

## 3. Respect the kerf — every single cut

Each pass of the blade removes real material ([here's the full story on kerf](/guides/what-is-kerf)). A row of parts needs one kerf *between each pair*, so "six pieces fit exactly" on paper usually means five fit in the shop. Use your blade's measured kerf in every calculation, and never trust a layout that fits with zero slack.

## 4. Cut big to small, and keep offcuts usable

The order of cuts decides what your scrap looks like:

- **Break the sheet down with full-length rips or crosscuts first.** Long guillotine cuts produce large, rectangular remainders.
- **Take the biggest parts out of the sheet first.** A big part cut late forces awkward cuts through otherwise-usable territory.
- **Aim for one large offcut instead of three slivers.** A 300 × 1200 remainder is a drawer side next month; three 100 mm strips are kindling. When two layouts waste the same area, pick the one whose waste is one solid rectangle.

Label offcuts with thickness and material as they come off the saw and store them where you'll look before opening a new sheet. An offcut you can't find is waste with extra steps.

## 5. Let the optimizer try the layouts you won't

Hand layout tops out fast: with a dozen parts, the number of reasonable arrangements is far beyond patience. An optimizer tries orderings and rotations systematically, applies kerf perfectly, honors your grain locks, and reports the waste percentage of the best plan it finds — in less time than sharpening a pencil.

Two practical tips when using one:

- **Compare objectives.** Fewest sheets, least waste, and fewest cuts are different goals; on some jobs they give different layouts. Fewest sheets saves money, fewest cuts saves an afternoon.
- **Re-run after every change.** One resized part can reshuffle an entire sheet for the better. Re-planning costs nothing; not re-planning costs plywood.

## 6. Accept the honest minimum

Some waste is structural. Grain-locked panels, an awkward part just over half the sheet width, edge trim — these put a floor under your waste number no matter what any tool promises. The goal isn't 0%; it's making sure every wasted square centimeter was *forced* by the design, not by the layout.
